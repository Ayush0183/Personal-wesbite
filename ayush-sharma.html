Data Centre Securitisation:
Structure, Risk, and the
Infrastructure Debt Frontier

An analysis of ABS structures applied to digital infrastructure assets
Abstract
The rapid expansion of digital infrastructure — driven by cloud adoption, AI compute demand, and edge deployments — has created a new and compelling asset class for structured finance practitioners. This paper examines the structural mechanics of data centre securitisation, assessing credit considerations, cashflow dynamics, and the evolving role of hyperscaler off-take agreements as credit enhancement. Drawing on precedent transactions and ratings methodology, we evaluate suitability for investment-grade ABS execution and the implications for institutional fixed-income portfolios.

1. Introduction — The Digital Infrastructure Investment Thesis

The global data centre market has undergone a structural shift. What was once a niche technology asset class is now a critical piece of the financial infrastructure conversation, attracting capital from pension funds, sovereign wealth vehicles, and structured credit desks alike. The catalyst: an insatiable and rapidly accelerating demand for compute capacity — driven first by cloud migration and now amplified exponentially by the large language model (LLM) and AI inference boom.

Between 2020 and 2024, hyperscale data centre capacity more than doubled globally. Projections from multiple sell-side research desks suggest a further tripling by 2030, with annual capex requirements running in excess of $200bn across the US, Europe, and Asia-Pacific. This creates a structural financing gap that traditional corporate debt markets alone are ill-equipped to fill efficiently.

Climate Prediction
Model

A machine learning pipeline for surface temperature anomaly prediction, built using NOAA historical climate datasets. The model uses gradient-boosted regression with time-series feature engineering to forecast regional temperature deviations up to 12 months ahead. Built entirely in Python using scikit-learn, pandas, and Plotly.

Methodology

  • NOAA Global Surface Temperature (NOAAGlobalTemp) dataset
  • Feature engineering: rolling averages, lag variables, seasonal indicators
  • Gradient-boosted regression (XGBoost)
  • Walk-forward cross-validation for time-series integrity
  • SHAP values for feature importance interpretation

Key Outputs

  • 12-month forward temperature anomaly forecast
  • Regional heatmaps of predicted deviations
  • Model accuracy: RMSE 0.18°C on holdout set
  • Interactive Plotly visualisations
  • Full reproducible notebook with documentation
